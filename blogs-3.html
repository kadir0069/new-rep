What To Expect During an MRI Scan

April 25, 2025 Dr. David Rodriguez

What Is an MRI?


MRI stands for Magnetic Resonance Imaging. Unlike X-rays or CT scans, it doesn’t use radiation. Instead, it uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images of organs, tissues, and bones.


MRIs are commonly used to examine:


  • The brain and spinal cord
  • Joints and soft tissues
  • The heart and blood vessels
  • Organs such as the liver, kidneys, and uterus


    • Preparing for the Scan


    1. Follow Instructions

    Your healthcare provider may give you specific instructions. For example, you may need to avoid eating or drinking a few hours before the scan if it's focused on your abdomen.

    2. Remove Metal Objects

    Because MRI machines use magnets, you’ll be asked to remove all metal items – jewelry, belts, watches, glasses, hearing aids, etc. Let your doctor know if you have any metal implants, pacemakers, or other devices.

    3. Wear Comfortable Clothing

    You might be asked to change into a hospital gown, but wearing loose, metal-free clothing can help keep things simple.


    What Happens During the Scan


    Here’s what you can expect once you arrive at the imaging center:


    1. Check-In and Pre-Scan Questions

    You’ll check in and complete a short questionnaire about your medical history and any implants or devices in your body.


    2. Getting Into Position

    You’ll lie down on a narrow table that slides into the MRI machine. Depending on the body part being scanned, the technician may use cushions or straps to help you stay still.


    3. The Scan Begins

    Once you’re inside the machine, you’ll hear loud tapping, thumping, or knocking sounds. These noises are completely normal and part of the machine’s operation. You’ll be given earplugs or headphones to reduce the noise.



    How Long Does It Take?


    Most MRI scans take between 15 to 45 minutes, depending on the part of the body being examined and whether contrast dye is used. Some complex scans may take up to an hour or more.



    After the MRI


  • You can go home and return to your normal activities immediately unless you were given a sedative.
  • If contrast dye was used, you may be asked to stay for a short period to ensure there are no reactions.
  • A radiologist will review your images and send a report to your doctor, who will then discuss the results with you.


    • Tips to Stay Comfortable


  • Practice deep breathing before the scan if you feel anxious.
  • Ask for music – some MRI centers offer headphones with your choice of music.
  • Let the staff know if you’re claustrophobic; open MRI options or mild sedatives may be available.


    • Final Thoughts

    An MRI scan is a safe and painless procedure that plays a vital role in modern diagnostics. By knowing what to expect, you can walk into your appointment feeling calm and confident. Don’t hesitate to ask questions – your care team is there to support you every step of the way.
